T1 — Trackless hairlines · 200×112, 120×92 (no growth)
9.F "NO scoring/progress bars with filled background tracks" · 7 density 8-10 "no card boxes; 1px lines separate data" · 4.2 Colour Consistency Lock
The filled grey track is the thing to kill. It exists to say "here is the maximum", but the card is a fixed width, so the maximum is already the card. Delete the track, leave a 1px hairline as the scale, and let a 3px accent mark ride it. Three of these cost less ink than one of the current bars, which is exactly why all three fit in 112px without touching the height. One accent for all three: the label says which window it is, colour has nothing to add. My earlier violet bar broke that lock for no reason.

T2 — Mono ledger · 200×112, 120×92 (no growth)
7 density 8-10 "font-mono for all numbers" mandatory · 4.4 "group with divide-y or negative space, not cards"
Numbers are the design. Each window is a ledger row: name left, value right in 15px mono, and the bar demoted to a 2px accent underline sitting directly beneath its own number, sized to the percentage. The bar stops being a separate object competing for width and becomes typographic weight on the number it belongs to. Hairlines between rows, no card inside the card. Honest weakness: the underline is only as wide as the number, roughly 30px, so it reads as a trend, not a measurement. Works because the number is right there.

T3 — Vertical column meters · 200×112, 120×92 (no growth)
4.4 "cards only when elevation communicates hierarchy" · 4.3 anti-centre bias · 7 density 9
Rotate the problem 90 degrees. Three horizontal bars fight for the card's width, which is the scarce axis; three vertical tubes fight for height, which is free once the bars leave the left column. The hero number gets the whole left side at 30px, bigger than today's 26px, and the three meters cluster right like a mixing desk. Height is what a percentage is easiest to read against, and 44px of tube beats 4px of bar for the same footprint. Costs you per-window numbers: the tubes are shape only, so you trade exact values for instant relative comparison.

T4 — Asymmetric split · 200×112, 120×92 (no growth)
7 DESIGN_VARIANCE 4-7 "left-aligned headers over centre-aligned data, varied ratios" · grid-template-columns 1.15fr 1fr
A 1.15:1 grid instead of the current label-plus-bar rows. Session owns the left cell at 32px with nothing competing; the two weekly windows stack in the right cell as trackless marks with their numbers set above them, not beside them. Putting the number above its own mark buys back the ~34px the right-hand percentage column was eating, so each mark still gets ~85px of scale despite living in half a card. The deliberate imbalance is the point: two of these three numbers are not equally urgent and the layout should say so.

T5 — Edge-bleed lanes · 200×112, 120×92 (no growth)
7 density 8-10 "tight paddings, no card boxes" · 4.4 Shape Consistency Lock (lanes inherit the card's 10px clip, no radius of their own)
The lanes run edge to edge and get clipped by the card's own corner radius instead of floating inside the 13px padding with their own rounded ends. That recovers 26px of real width per lane, the widest scale of anything in either set, and it stops the bars reading as three little pills glued onto a panel. They read as the panel's own instrumentation. The keys sit above as a single mono strip so no label column steals width. Riskiest one here: bleeding to a clipped edge only looks intentional if the corner radius is exact, and a half-pixel of WebView2 antialiasing on a transparent window will show.
